Voronoi Diagrams on the GPU

Disclaimer: the demos on this page use WebGL features that aren't available on many mobile devices. For the best experience, read this on a Desktop computer.

What is a Voronoi diagram? Try the demo below to find out. When you click in the left canvas, you plant a colored 'seed'. In the right canvas, every pixel takes the color of the closest seed.

Here's another demo that's slightly more interactive. Try moving over the canvasses below.

Here's one more example. In the left canvas there are seeds in a radial pattern overlaid on top of a photo. Each seed takes the color of the underlying pixel in the image.

Move the circle to move the seeds and move the square to rotate them.

How does this work?

There's a really simple way of computing Voronoi diagrams: for every pixel in an image, look through every seed and take the color of the closest one. This gets pretty slow as you add a lot of seeds.

There are a few other popular algorithms for computing Voronoi diagrams, and I won't go into all of them. But I was recently showed one that I thought was particularly cool. It's performance doesn't depend on how many seeds you start with - and it's well suited to running on the GPU. It ends up pretty fast, and it's possible to compute Voronoi diagrams in real time.
